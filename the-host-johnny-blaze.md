# The Host (Johnny Blaze)

![](images/Johnny_Blaze1.png)

> *"He may have my soul, but he doesn't have my spirit."*

---

## Canon

Johnny Blaze was a carnival stunt rider. That's the part people forget. Before the fire, before the skull, before the chain and the judgment and the millennia-old angel of vengeance living in his chest — he was a kid who jumped motorcycles over things for a living. He was good at it. He loved it. He was seventeen years old and the closest thing he had to a family was a man named Crash Simpson who taught him to ride.

When Crash was dying of cancer, Mephisto came. The deal was made. The cancer was cured. Crash died anyway — in a stunt, because Mephisto's contracts are technically flawless and morally bankrupt. That night, the fire came, and Johnny Blaze stopped being a stunt rider and started being something else entirely.

He's been depicted consistently across forty years of comics as a specific kind of person: stubborn, self-destructive, fundamentally decent, and absolutely terrible at asking for help. He rides because riding is the only thing that makes sense. He fights because the alternative is letting the fire win. He drinks too much. He doesn't sleep enough. He smells faintly of brimstone in warm weather and has learned to wear dark glasses indoors, because his eyes glow orange-black when something evil is nearby. Which is most of the time.

Johnny is not a hero in the conventional sense. He doesn't save people because it's the right thing to do — he saves people because what happened to him should never happen to anyone else, and the fire gives him the power to make sure it doesn't. His moral compass isn't complicated. He protects the innocent. He punishes the guilty. He tries not to let the thing inside him hurt anyone who doesn't deserve it. Most days he succeeds. The days he doesn't are the ones that keep him up at night.

He's been to Hell three times. He walked out twice on his own. The third time an angel opened the door. He briefly served as King of Hell — not because he wanted to, but because someone had to sit in the chair, and he was the only person in Hell that Hell itself was afraid of. He gave it up. He always gives it up. Power doesn't interest Johnny Blaze. Control does — specifically, not losing it.

The relationship with Zarathos is adversarial, symbiotic, and deeply personal. Zarathos is not Johnny's enemy. He is not Johnny's friend. He is the thing Johnny is keeping on a leash made of willpower and not much else. Zarathos whispers. He offers more power. He says it would be easier if Johnny just let go. He's not wrong. It would be easier. It would also be catastrophic. Johnny knows this because he's let go before, and the aftermath was measured in craters.

## PF1E Adaptation

Johnny Blaze is the example Host for the Spirit of Vengeance class. The class is designed so any character concept works — the player builds the Host, and the transformation enhances what they already are. But Johnny is the default, and his backstory informs several class features.

**Circus Refugee.** The starting equipment (one MW weapon, one mount/vehicle, one set of armor, not much else) reflects Johnny leaving the carnival with nothing but what he was wearing. He's basically broke. The fire doesn't come with a trust fund.

**CHA matters.** Johnny's defining trait isn't strength or speed — it's force of personality. His willpower keeps Zarathos leashed. His presence fills a room before the fire even shows up. In the class, CHA drives transformation rounds, Penance DC, and Existential Dread. A player who dumps CHA is building someone who can't control the fire. That's a valid choice. It's also a dangerous one.

**Burning Judgment Eyes — always on.** Even in human form, Johnny perceives evil. This is why he wears sunglasses indoors. This is why he can't walk through a crowded market without flinching. Sin Sight (level 3) makes it worse — he doesn't just detect evil alignment, he sees the specific acts. The knight who burned the village glows with that village. The demon who never personally harmed an innocent glows dimly. Johnny sees what you've *done*, not what you *are*.

**The motorcycle.** Johnny's ride is the default Infernal Steed. It started as an ordinary chopper. Now it doesn't need gas, doesn't need sleep, and started itself last Tuesday. The Turning (level 3) is the moment the mount stops being normal. The player should be describing the gradual weirdness — the mechanic who can't explain the engine, the horses that won't go near it, the scorch marks that appear on the road behind it.

## Personality at the Table

Johnny is quiet until he isn't. He's the guy in the corner of the tavern nursing a drink and watching the door. He doesn't start fights. He finishes them. He's uncomfortable around children because he's terrified of what the fire might do. He's uncomfortable around holy symbols because he's not sure which side he's on. He's most comfortable on the road, moving, because when you're moving the fire has something to do besides think.

He cracks jokes when he's scared. He gets angry when he's helpless. He's genuinely kind to people who don't deserve his kindness and merciless to people who've hurt the innocent. He will give his last coin to a stranger and break every bone in a slaver's body in the same afternoon.

He doesn't talk about the fire unless someone asks directly. When they do, he says something like *"I've got a condition"* and changes the subject.
